How to Get around

Just Maps Mode: Maps of Nashville and Davidson County from 1871, 1903, 1929 and 1952, with the option to overlay a modern (2016) map for comparison.

Landmarks: Info and photos of historical buildings and other structures in existence at the time of the selected map.

Streets: The origin of street names where known, former names where applicable, and some historical and geographic trivia.

Battle of Nashville: A static map of the fortiﬁcations, earthworks and troop positions on December 15th, 1864, along with a modern map overlay.

The Concept

Having an interest in history, questions would occur to me as I drove around my city. What sed to occupy that vacant lot? Why does this street make a sudden turn? Wat did this eighborhood look like 1000 years ago?

Atlases, and especially historical atlases, normally take the world, a country, or occasionally a state or province as their scope, but every place no matter the size has its own story. What would a historical atlas of a city look like? What if the city getting that detailed historical treatment was a mid-size Southern city instead of a coastal metropolis? What would a more holistic, graphic and map-based approach to local history look like?

The Making of

The atlas began way back in 2007 with a pair of maps drawn in Macromedia Freehand, a 2006-ish modern map and a 1952 map, and a rudimentary UI created in Flash. Adobe’s disconinuation of Freehand and Steve Jobs’s 2010 Flash-killing public letter, "Thoughts on Flash", discouraged that particular vision and put the project on hold for a few years.

Work began again in 2015. The original maps were imported into Illustrator and reﬁned. Over the next ﬁve years the 2006 modern map was updated to 2016; the 1929, 1903, 1871 and 1864 maps were created; and the UI was redesigned a number of times.

Drawing the 1871, 1903, and 1929 maps involved a surprising amount of interpretation and even guesswork for three reasons:

  • These maps were based on period maps that had been drawn without the beneﬁt of aerial photography and so were not uniformly accurate.
  • In rural areas 19th- and early-20th-century roads roads moved around quite a bit and sometimes vanished without leaving a trace on modern maps.
  • Maps from roughly the same time often contradict each other. Streets, roads and even whole neighborhoods depicted on one map will be absent on another, or will have diﬀerent names, requiring a modern mapmaker relying on those maps to make editorial judgements.

The partial solution was to painstakingly compare the maps drawn in Illustrator to historical aerial photographs. Abandoned public roads usually leave marks on the land, whether as trails, farm roads,fencelines, hedgerows, or just lines of trees in a ﬁeld, but modern development brings bulldozers that typically erase all traces of history, at least those visible from the air.

Luckily for this project, most of Davidson County had been photographed in the early 1950s, capturing the state of aﬀairs just before the building of the interstates, the spread of subdivisions, the expansion of the airport, and the ﬂooding of Percy Priest Lake. Much of 2020 was devoted to checking the 1871, 1903, and 1929 maps against old aerial photography and making corrections.

In late 2021 a developer, Amrit Karmacharya, was hired and the ﬁrst map-only phase of development began, resulting by the end of the year in a set of tiled online maps that could be displayed with or without the modern overlay.

With the maps done, the focus in 2022 shifted to creating the street and landmark content. The websites of the Tennessee Secretary of State and the Nashville Public Library make thousands of historical images available to the public, and these websites provided most of the images used in this project. Researching and writing the capsule histories of streets, buildings and bridges required a full bookshelf of books on local history books and numerous online sources. The Nashville History blog (https://nashvillehistory.blogspot.com/) stood out for the breadth of its subject matter and the depth of its research.

As of this writing (March 2023) development has reached its ﬁnal phase, and the site will be migrated to its own domain soon. Planned enhancements include an interactive Battle of Nashville map and an additional historical map, probably depicting a year around 1980.
